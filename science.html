Randomness

By far the most interesting part about generating a mirror is the way the curve is generated. It is amazing that through random motion one can create a mirror far superior to what most machines can create. While true randomness is hard to come by, the psuedorandomness of our mirror grinding is good enough. To go over why this works, one has to know the general process of grinding a curve.

The general process for grinding or "hogging" a curve is using the "chordal" stroke. It is named as such because the focus of the stroke is making the mirror (which is on top) follow a "chord" of the tool (on bottom). With about 40% of the mirror hanging off the side, you follow a chord on the tool away and towards you, while putting pressure on the mirror. Every 5-10 strokes (full back and forth) you turn the mirror a bit (anywhere from 20-80 degrees). Every 1-6 of turning the mirror, you turn the tool in the opposite direction. This may seem a strange process to some. The center of the mirror is always in contact with the edge of the tool and being ground. Just next to the center of the mirror is being ground not always, but almost all of the time. Slightly farther out is being ground slightly less. Once we get to the edge of the mirror, which is only in contact with the tool a fraction of the time that the center is. Over thousands and thousands of these strokes, the random motion generates an incredibly accurate spherical curve.

Now because we are using randomness to generate our curve, we must ensure that any process we perform is not dictated by habit or procedure. The only rule is to not make more rules. We want everything to be random, to the amount hanging off of the tool, the number of strokes before we turn the mirror, strokes before we turn tool, the pressure we apply to the mirror, and any other factor that can be introduced.
